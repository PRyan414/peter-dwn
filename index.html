Six Core Themes
Reading the pattern beneath the patterns

Across all 32 HMW questions, six recurring design challenges emerge for Peter. These aren't problems to solve — they're terrains to navigate.

Reading the Map
Peter's Design Challenge
The tension between who he is and how he holds himself back. Updated with new material from Peter's Miro boards on identity, reinvention, and curiosity.
Theme 01
The Inner Critic as Gatekeeper
Self-doubt is operating as a permission-granting authority across nearly every domain — impact, authenticity, love, health. The design challenge: demote it from decision-maker to one voice among many.
Theme 02
Inherited Rules vs. Chosen Values
Limiting beliefs show up most strongly around fairness, rest, and readiness. Many appear to be borrowed frameworks — someone else's rules installed in Peter's life. The design challenge: audit and rewrite the rulebook.
Theme 03
Waiting for Certainty
Fear of wrong decisions and resistance to change cluster around transitions, relationships, and growth. The design challenge: shift from seeking the right answer to running the right experiments.
Theme 04
Self-Compassion Deficit
Peter's value of fairness coexists with a striking unfairness toward himself — in self-talk, in standards, in rest. The design challenge: make fairness as internal as it is external.
Theme 05 — from Miro
Identity Beyond the Role
"Without my current role, who would I be?" is the most charged question on Peter's board. His identity appears tightly bound to his professional function. The design challenge: build a self-concept spacious enough to survive any job ending.
Theme 06 — from Miro
Curiosity as Unlived Life
Peter's curiosity map is vast — architecture, music, sailing, sustainable design, history, sourdough. None of these appear to be current pursuits. The design challenge: treat at least one curiosity as a serious prototype, not a someday.
The North Star HMW
"How might we help Peter build a life where being most himself is also how he contributes most — without waiting to be ready, and without needing his current role to tell him who he is?"
This single question holds all eight values, addresses all four patterns, and now integrates the identity and curiosity threads from Peter's Miro boards.
Cognitive Reframes
From stuck story to open question

Each of Peter's four recurring patterns can be reframed using the HMW move. These aren't affirmations — they're genuine questions that open up possibility space.

Stuck Story: Self-Doubt
"I'm not sure I'm good enough / capable enough / worthy enough to move forward."
Open Question
"How might I treat doubt as a research question rather than a final verdict?"
Stuck Story: Resistance to Change
"Change is destabilizing. If I move, I'll lose what I have. Better to wait."
Open Question
"How might I find what stays constant so I can move more freely with what changes?"
Stuck Story: Fear of Wrong Decision
"I need to be certain before I act. A wrong choice could cost me everything."
Open Question
"How might I design decisions as experiments — with review dates and low downside — so I can move sooner?"
Stuck Story: Limiting Beliefs
"This is just who I am / how things are / what I deserve. It's fixed."
Open Question
"How might I treat this belief as a hypothesis I inherited — and design an experiment to test whether it's actually true?"
Stuck Story: Identity Dependency — from Miro
"Without my current role, I don't know who I am or what I'd do. The blank canvas is terrifying."
Open Question
"How might I begin sketching the next version of me now — while I still have the safety of the current structure — so reinvention is a choice, not a crisis?"
Stuck Story: Deferred Curiosity — from Miro
"I have all these interests — architecture, sailing, music, sustainability — but I never really pursue them. Someday."
Open Question
"How might I treat one curiosity as a serious 30-day prototype — not a hobby, not a career pivot, just a real experiment in what it feels like to actually do it?"
The Burnett & Evans Reminder

"A well-designed life is a life that makes sense. It's a life in which who you are, what you believe, and what you do all line up together." The HMW question doesn't tell you where to go. It clears the ground so you can actually see where you already want to go.
